As psychologists, we use a variety of evidence-based therapeutic approaches to support our clients. One such approach is values clarification, a key component of Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT). 

Values and goal clarification help us understand what is most meaningful to our clients, identify the direction they want their lives to take, and guide therapy towards a life that is personally meaningful and fulfilling. The process often begins with a simple yet powerful question: "What are your values?"

Many people respond with values such as family, honesty, health, freedom, or kindness. These values provide valuable insight into what gives a person's life meaning and purpose. 

However, one recent therapeutic experience reminded me that values are not always just values. Sometimes, they are expressions of unmet psychological needs. 
Sometimes, they are reflections of unresolved trauma. And sometimes, they become the doorway to understanding the deepest beliefs a person holds about themselves, others, and the world.

# The Clinical Context

I was working with a client who presented with concerns related to **gender identity, gender expression, and sexual orientation** within the context of a previous diagnosis of **Gender Dysphoria**. During assessment, the client also described a history of multiple **Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs)**, including emotional abuse, bullying, physical punishment, humiliation, and repeated violations of personal boundaries by significant adults.

While these presenting concerns were important, I did not want therapy to begin by focusing solely on the diagnosis.

Instead, I wanted to understand the person sitting in front of me. Rather than asking, **"What is wrong with you?"** I became interested in asking, **"What matters most to you?"**

# Why I Chose ACT Values Clarification

One of the fundamental principles of **Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)** is helping individuals identify personally meaningful values that can guide behaviour and improve psychological flexibility.

Values clarification is not about identifying goals. Goals can be achieved. Values are ongoing directions in life. More importantly, values provide insight into the person's identity, aspirations, motivations, and sense of meaning.

Within a **trauma-informed and gender-affirming framework**, values clarification is particularly valuable because it allows clients to define what matters to them without the therapist imposing assumptions or predetermined narratives. Instead of making therapy about the diagnosis, the intervention makes therapy about the person.

The purpose of using ACT Values Clarification in this case was to explore:

* What gives this person's life meaning?
* What kind of person do they want to become?
* What needs have remained unmet throughout their life?
* How have early experiences influenced the values they hold today?

The client identified five core values:

🏋️ Fitness
🧭 Independence
⚖️ Justice
🕊️ Freedom
🧩 Order

![Desktop View](assets/valuve.jpg)

Initially, these appeared to be ordinary life values. However, something made me curious.

# A Simple Question Changed the Entire Session

Instead of asking, **"Why is this value important?"** I repeatedly asked one simple question. > **"What does that mean to you?"** Each response became the starting point for another question. > **"And if that were true, what would that mean to you?"** With each answer, the conversation moved one layer deeper. The values gradually transformed into personal meanings. The meanings transformed into unmet needs. The unmet needs eventually revealed deeply held core beliefs.

# Why I Used the Downward Arrow Technique

At this stage, I intentionally incorporated the **Downward Arrow Technique**, a cognitive conceptualisation method commonly used in **Cognitive Behaviour Therapy (CBT)**. The purpose of this technique is to move beyond surface-level thoughts and uncover the deeper assumptions and core beliefs that influence a person's emotions, behaviours, and interpretation of life events. Rather than asking **what** the client believed, the technique helped explore **why** those beliefs existed. 

Given the client's history of developmental trauma and adverse childhood experiences, I wanted to understand whether these values represented genuine life directions, unmet emotional needs, or deeply rooted beliefs that had developed as adaptive responses to painful experiences. What emerged was one of the most meaningful therapeutic moments I have experienced.

**Fitness**

**"I am not safe."**

Initially, fitness appeared to represent physical health. Instead, it represented psychological safety. The client's desire to become physically stronger reflected a longing to protect themselves from further abuse, humiliation, and vulnerability.

# Clinical Reflection

This experience reminded me that values often tell a much deeper story than we initially expect. Sometimes,

1. Fitness is really about safety.
2. Independence is really about competence.
3. Justice is really about protection.
4. Freedom is really about authenticity.
5. Order is really about identity.

By combining ACT Values Clarification with the Downward Arrow Technique, therapy moved beyond identifying values and towards understanding the developmental experiences that gave those values their meaning. Integrating these evidence-based approaches supported a trauma-informed, gender-affirming, and person-centred therapeutic process.

Rather than making assumptions about the client's identity or focusing exclusively on gender dysphoria, the therapeutic process centred on the client's lived experiences, personal meanings, and psychological needs. This collaborative approach respected the client's autonomy while recognising the influence of developmental trauma on identity development and psychological functioning.

Importantly, this process highlighted that the client's distress extended beyond gender-related concerns. The values and core beliefs pointed towards broader themes of safety, competence, justice, authenticity, and identity, all of which became meaningful targets for ongoing therapeutic work.

This session reinforced an important lesson: evidence-based practice is not about rigidly following a single therapeutic model. It is about thoughtfully selecting and integrating interventions that best meet the unique needs of the individual sitting in front of us.

One simple question "What does that mean to you?" asked with genuine curiosity and repeated with patience, can uncover a lifetime of unmet needs, deeply held core beliefs, and opportunities for healing.

At the same time, this experience reminded me that some of the most meaningful therapeutic moments emerge when therapists are willing to think beyond standard protocols, manuals. Clinical creativity, therapeutic flexibility, and the ability to integrate evidence-based interventions within a well-developed case formulation can profoundly influence the direction of a therapy session.

However, creativity should never replace formulation or evidence. Therapeutic techniques should not be applied simply because they are familiar or preferred. Instead, every intervention should be guided by a comprehensive case formulation, the client's presenting concerns, therapeutic goals, and the best available evidence. When evidence-based practice, sound clinical reasoning, case formulation, and therapeutic creativity come together, therapy becomes more than the application of techniques. It becomes a collaborative process of understanding, meaning-making, and healing.

This session was a powerful reminder that effective psychotherapy is not about rigidly following a manual. It is about understanding the unique person sitting in front of us, remaining curious about their lived experience, and thoughtfully selecting interventions that fit their needs. Sometimes, a therapist's curiosity, creativity, and ability to integrate evidence-based approaches in the moment can completely transform the outcome of a session.

The true art of psychotherapy lies not only in knowing what intervention to use, but also when, why, and how to use it—and, most importantly, with whom.
