The *Ambalaṭṭhika Rāhulovāda Sutta*, documented in the Majjhima Nikāya (MN 61), illustrates several significant psychotherapy and therapeutic elements. These elements can be understood within a framework that includes (1) developing a therapeutic alliance, (2) assessment and problem identification, (3) goal setting, (4) therapeutic interventions, (5) termination, and (6) follow-up.

**1\. Developing the Therapeutic Alliance**

The client, Rahula Thera, is a child. The relationship between the client and the psychologist (the Buddha) is presented as safe, respectful, and non-judgemental in this sutta. The psychologist approaches the child gently, accepts his hospitality, and speaks calmly, without criticism, punishment, or blame. This shows boundary settings and rapport building from the beginning of the process. The psychologist was firm but compassionate and aims to modify maladaptive behavior response patterns early that could be psycho-social problems in his later life.

The therapeutic relationship forms the foundation for the later stages of the therapy process. This relationship is built on truth, honesty, and conscience. Therefore, therapy can be effective only when the client is honest with both himself and the psychologist. Dishonesty, whether towards oneself or others, disrupts the therapy process, as lying may open the way for further psychological and behavioral problems. In this sutta, the psychologist initiates a dialogue-based and emotionally safe conversation, allowing the child to learn gradually from his own experience.

This approach reflects key qualities of a therapist such as empathy, unconditional positive regard, genuineness, compassion (karuṇā), openness, and approachability. It shows that effective therapy process begins with a strong therapeutic relationship.

**2\. Assessment and Problem Identification**

Instead of directly labelling the client's behavior as wrong, the psychologist uses metaphorical stories to provide psychoeducation of his behaviour patterns.

-   The metaphors of *the little water in the pot* illustrated that when a person lies, their goodness becomes very small, and lying destroys honesty and trust.

-   *Throwing away the water* shows repeatedly lying to oneself and others throws away one's moral and ethical conduct, and a liar loses conscience.

-   *The pot turned upside down* showed lying turns a person's life upside down; it distorted his cognition and perception and changed his values. Wrong is justified as right. It leads to motivation for change and discrepancy between 'who one wants to be' and 'what one becomes through lying.'

-   *The empty or hollow pot* shows a liar gradually becoming empty inside, and no real meaning of life or goodness remains.

This is an age-appropriate method that helps the child to recognize his own problem of deliberate lying and its short- and long-term consequences.

In order to foster gradual awareness, the Buddha has used guided self-discovery, Socratic questioning, or open-ended questions to reflect his own behavioral misconduct through these stories. This dialogue related to a mirror metaphor to further explain the importance of self-reflection. Like the way we see this world through our eyes, we cannot see our whole body with our naked eyes. Therefore, we use a mirror to see those areas. As the same as the psychologist, the Buddha here played a role as a mirror that showed Rahula's unseen behavioral disinhibition in a self-reflective and observational way. It is an insight-focused assessment, where the client is guided to understand the impact of his own behavior without a sense of labelling, discrimination, and shame or guilt.

The royal war elephant metaphor has been used to show the consequences of lying. As long as a person does not lie, they still have a commitment to protect their limits. When a person does not have self-control to lie, they lose all their limits. Once they lie, that becomes normal; repeatedly lying or other harmful actions also become very easy for them. However, truthfulness protects a person, which is like the royal war elephant protecting his trunk during a fight, which symbolizes his truthfulness and honesty. As a consequence, the Buddha focuses on understanding why Rāhula lied rather than only the act of lying. He recognizes that, as a child, Rāhula had not yet developed proper moral shame (*hiri*) and that his thinking was influenced by mental defilements (*kilesa*), leading to cognitive biases.

**3\. Goal Setting**

Once the problem is identified, the Buddha, as the psychologist, clearly defines the therapeutic goals. The main goals are to rebuild truthfulness and to purify actions of body, speech, and mind (*sīla*). For instance, the Buddha encourages the development of honesty (*sacca*) through reflection, acceptance, and self-compassion. He sets a clear behavioral goal, "I will not tell a lie, even as a joke," which helps promote behavioral modification.

This approach reflects psychotherapy practices that emphasize clear, meaningful, and ethically based goals rather than unclear or complicated ones. The therapeutic goals help align Rāhula's behavior with his authentic identity as a monk and guide him towards living according to his values. In this way, the goals support the symbolic idea of turning the pot upright again and filling it with moral-value-based water. Furthermore, healing is understood as coming through personal responsibility. Taking responsibility for one's own behaviour helps maintain positive behavioural changes and supports gradual mental development and cognitive transformation.

**4\. Intervention**

The main therapeutic intervention is the practice of self-reflection on Rāhula's own actions before, during, and after acting.

i. Before acting, Rāhula is encouraged to ask himself:\
*"Will this action cause harm to myself or others?"*

a. If the answer is yes, he should not do it.\
b. If the answer is no, it is appropriate to proceed.

ii. During the action, he is advised to observe whether any harm is occurring.

a. If harm is noticed, the action should be stopped immediately.

iii. After the action, reflection is again encouraged:

a. If harm has occurred, Rāhula is advised to acknowledge it, confess to a teacher or wise person, and try not to repeat the same action.\
b. If no harm has occurred, he is encouraged to feel happy and continue engaging in wholesome actions.

This structured, stage-wise approach supports behavioral modification (*sīla*) intervention through the domains of body (what Rahula does), speech (what Rahula says), and mind (what Rahula thinks).

In addition, when harm occurs, Rāhula is encouraged to feel appropriate regret and learn from his mistakes without being shamed or overly self-critical. This process helps develop mental calmness and stability (*samādhi*), supporting gradual mental development. Moreover, Rāhula is guided to reflect on his intentions and the consequences of his own actions, which helps him recognize and correct unhelpful ways of thinking. This reflective understanding leads to the development of wisdom (*paññā*) and cognitive transformation.

In order to help Rāhula develop gradual self-awareness, the Buddha, acting as the psychologist, uses a powerful therapeutic intervention known as mirroring (reflecting back the client's thoughts, feelings, or behavior). He explains that just as a mirror is used to see one's face, self-reflection should be used to see one's actions. This metaphor presents the psychologist as a mirror, supporting the client in developing self-awareness and metacognition (thinking about Rahula's own thinking), rather than giving advice or creating dependence on the psychologist.

**5\. Termination**

The Buddha gradually places responsibility on Rāhula, encouraging him to reflect independently and regulate his behavior ethically. This reflects an effective termination phase in psychotherapy, in which the client internalizes the skills learned and no longer depends on the psychologist for guidance. In this sutta, termination is not unexpected; rather, it occurs when Rāhula is effectively prepared to manage his conduct independently, with insight and confidence.

**6\. Follow-Up**

Finally, the Buddha emphasizes continuous practice, encouraging Rāhula to train "day and night" in wholesome qualities. This reflects the follow-up and maintenance phase of psychotherapy, which focuses on relapse prevention and long-term personal development. As depicted in this sutta, psychotherapy is presented as a lifelong process in which reflection and ethical awareness are continually reminded, rather than being limited to formal psychotherapy sessions.

**Conclusion**

In conclusion, the Ambalaṭṭhika Rāhulovāda Sutta (MN 61) presents a clear and structured Buddhist-based psychotherapy approach that aligns with Western psychotherapy concepts, making it easier to explain and understand the psychotherapy elements and processes depicted in this sutta. In this sutta, the Buddha supports Rāhula in understanding the causes of his behavior in a non-judgmental and age-appropriate manner, using dialogue-based discussions, compassion, age-appropriate psychoeducation, and guided self-reflection to foster insight and personal growth consistent with his role as a monk. Through clear goals and practical interventions, this Buddhism-based psychotherapy process promotes truthfulness, ethical conduct, emotional regulation, and wisdom, while gradually encouraging the client's autonomy. These elements demonstrate that this sutta covers core therapeutic principles that are highly relevant to psychotherapy practice. The emphasis on continuous practice highlights psychotherapy as a lifelong process that supports lasting insight, ethical awareness, and overall well-being.
